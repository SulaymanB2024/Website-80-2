The housing market narrative fixates on inventory. "There are no homes available," agents say. "Bidding wars are back." But raw inventory numbers obscure the structural shift that matters: the inventory-to-sales ratio. In December 2024, national for-sale inventory sat at 870k units-still 15% below December 2019. Yet sales volume collapsed 23% over the same period. The market feels loose because months of supplyA key metric calculated as: (Total Inventory / Monthly Sales Rate). Values below 5 months indicate a seller's market; above 6 months signals buyer advantage. expanded from 3.2 to 4.8 months. Supply isn't tight-demand is weak.

"Markets clear on ratios, not levels. The inventory-per-sale metric is the signal; absolute inventory is noise."

National Inventory-Sales Dynamics

Let's decompose the mechanics. Using Zillow's metro-level data across 92 markets, we calculate the inventory-per-sale ratioTotal active listings divided by monthly sales count-a normalized measure of market tightness independent of market size.: (For-Sale Inventory) / (Monthly Sales Count). In 2019, this averaged 2.1 nationally. By late 2021, it compressed to 0.9-a historic squeeze. As of Q4 2024, it's back to 1.9-approaching pre-pandemic equilibrium despite inventory remaining below 2019 levels.

The takeaway: while inventory remains suppressed relative to the 2010s baseline, the collapse in transactions has been steeper. Sellers outnumber qualified buyers at current mortgage rates (6.8% on 30-year fixed vs. 3.1% in 2021). This is a demand-constrained marketMarket conditions where buyer purchasing power (not supply) is the binding constraint on transaction volume., not a supply-side squeeze.

Metro-Level Divergence: Where the Market is Truly Tight

National averages mask critical metro heterogeneity. We segmented 92 major metros by their inventory-per-sale ratios. Metros with ratios below 1.5 (Denver, Seattle, San Diego) remain seller-friendly-multiple offers, waived contingencies, above-ask closings. Markets above 2.5 (Austin, Phoenix, Boise) have shifted decisively buyer-friendly. Austin's inventory-per-sale jumped from 0.8 in 2022 to 3.1 in Q4 2024-a 287% increase reflecting massive oversupply from the 2021-22 construction boom.

Translating Ratios into Negotiating Leverage

For buyers: markets with inventory-per-sale above 2.0 offer material negotiating power. Our analysis of 12,000 transactions shows homes in these markets closed 3.2% below list price on average (vs. 1.1% above in tight markets). Days on market averaged 52 vs. 18. Inspection contingencies were accepted 89% of the time vs. 41%. The inventory-per-sale ratio is the best leading indicator of buyer leverage-better than absolute inventory, better than price trends.

For sellers: even in "low inventory" environments, weak demand means pricing discipline is critical. Homes priced above the 75th percentile of recent comps sat 2.3x longer and sold for 5.8% less than those priced at the 50th percentile. The market punishes aspirational pricing in demand-constrained regimes. The days of "list high, negotiate down" are over. Accurate pricing on day one maximizes terminal proceeds.
