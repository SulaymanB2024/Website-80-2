The American housing market is experiencing an unprecedented affordability wedgeThe divergence between the income required to purchase a home versus the income required to rent-a measure of buy-rent cost arbitrage.. Using Zillow's homeownership affordability index and rent data across 92 metros, we calculate that the median income needed to buy a typical home has surged 79% since 2019, reaching $106,500 annually (assuming 20% down, 30-year fixed at 6.8%, 28% DTI ratio). Meanwhile, the income needed to afford median rent (using a 30% income threshold) rose just 37% to $62,800. This 69% buy-rent income premium is the highest in modern data history, eclipsing even the 2006 peak.

"When the math to rent is half the math to buy, prices must fall or rents must rise. There is no third option."

Decomposing the Wedge: Rates, Prices, and Rents

Three variables drove the wedge expansion:

1. Mortgage rates tripled: From 3.1% (Dec 2021) to 6.8% (Dec 2024). Monthly payment on a $400k loan jumped from $1,710 to $2,590-a 51% increase with zero price appreciation.
2. Home prices remained elevated: Despite corrections in some metros, national median home values are still 42% above 2019 ($358k vs. $252k).
3. Rents rose but decelerated: National median rent grew 37% (2019→2024) but has flatlined since mid-2022, constrained by tenant income limits and new supply in sunbelt metros.

The implication: at current prices and rates, homeownership requires household incomes 69% higher than renting. For context, in 2019 the premium was just 30%-barely above historical norms. The 2024 wedge is structurally unprecedented.

Metro-Level Analysis: Where the Wedge is Worst

We ranked 92 metros by their buy-rent income ratio. The worst offenders-where buying requires 2x or more the income of renting-are concentrated in high-cost coastal markets and pandemic boomtowns where prices spiked but rents plateaued. Austin, TX tops the list: $135k income to buy vs. $58k to rent (2.3x ratio). This reflects oversupply in the rental market (new multifamily completions up 87% YoY) compressing rents while mortgage rates keep home prices sticky.

Why Rents Are the Pressure Valve

Rents are income-constrainedRents cannot exceed tenant income capacity-a hard ceiling that prices do not face due to down payment flexibility and rate buydowns.. Unlike home prices, which can disconnect from incomes via leverage, rents are bounded by what tenants can pay from monthly wages. When rents hit 35-40% of median household income, demand destruction occurs-people double up, move to cheaper metros, or accept longer commutes. This creates a natural ceiling.

We see this constraint binding in real-time. Metros where rent-to-income ratios exceeded 38% in 2022-23 (Miami, Austin, Phoenix) have seen rent growth stall or reverse as new supply flooded the market. Developers responded to high rents by building aggressively; those units are now competing for tenants whose incomes didn't keep pace. Meanwhile, home prices remain elevated because existing homeowners are "rate-locked" at 3% mortgages and won't sell. The wedge persists because supply responses differ: elastic on the rental side, frozen on the for-sale side.

Investment Implications: Price Mean Reversion or Rent Acceleration?

The wedge must close. Two paths:

1. Home prices fall 15-20%: This requires forced selling-recession, job losses, or rate-lock breaking via life events (divorce, relocation, death). Absent a catalyst, sticky supply keeps prices elevated.
2. Rents rise 20-25%: This requires wage growth (unlikely at that pace) or tenant income stretching beyond historical norms. Also constrained by supply: multifamily completions remain elevated through 2025.

The most probable scenario: time heals the wedge. Inflation erodes real home prices while nominal rents grind higher. If home prices stay flat and rents grow 4% annually, the wedge closes in 6-7 years. But metros with extreme wedges (>2.0x ratio) face heightened risk of price corrections if mortgage rates stay elevated or if a recession forces distressed sales. For investors: underweight high-wedge markets for appreciation; overweight for rental yield and distressed acquisition opportunities.
